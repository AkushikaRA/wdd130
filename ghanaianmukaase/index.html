History of Ghanaian food

Learn More about Ghana's History and it's cuisines

Ghanaian cuisine is varied. It is a result of the region's geography, history, and inhabitants. To put it simply, Ghanaian cuisine consists primarily of hot soups and stews that are created with a lot of tomatoes, onions, pepper, ginger, and other locally grown spices. Stews are thick and frequently served with starchy tubers like plantains or the West African yam. Light soups are served with a variety of starchy sides made from tubers including corn, cassava, cocoyam, or West African yam. The Sahel region of southern Africa is contacted by the northern half of Ghana. Grassland and deciduous trees cover the land, which is arid yet still abundant. The majority of Ghana's food supply is raised above the transition zone. This comprises most crops cultivated in forested areas as well as rice, tubers, millet, fonio, guinea corn, vegetables, income crops like shea butter, and superfoods like baobab and moringa. It also contains cash crops like shea butter. The country's southern region is heavily forested, muggy, and damp. Because of this, you'll find plenty of fresh fruits, vegetables, plantains, tubers (cassava, cocoyam), and oil palm. The majority of Ghana's cacao is also grown in these areas. Did you know Ghana exports more cacao beans than any other country? So now you are aware.
